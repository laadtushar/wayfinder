# Wayfinder: building an explorable-worlds game on Galaxy XR
## The year is mid-2026 — the headset, the engine, the store, and even on-device photoreal capture already ship. The frontier isn't the platform; it's the world-generation pipeline and the AI in the world.

This is the "what's actually new and useful, validated across sources" document for your plan: use SLAM3R-style reconstruction, plus Unity/Unreal, to build a Star-Trek-flavoured planet-exploration game on the Samsung Galaxy XR. I split it into four tiers by adoption risk — Use-today, Use-soon, Bet-on, Speculative — and for each item I say what it is, why it matters *for your specific build*, the concrete plumbing, and the catch. It was built from ~40 web searches across seven angles (platform, engines, publishing, neural reconstruction, Gaussian splatting, generative worlds, and the Star Trek IP question), then an adversarial fact-check pass over 43 load-bearing claims (35 confirmed, 8 corrected, 0 false). Where a number is a rule-of-thumb rather than a measured benchmark, I say so.

Three reframes before the tiers, because they change what you build:

**Reframe 1 — SLAM3R does not run on the headset, and neither does anything in its family.** Every feed-forward reconstruction model (SLAM3R, DUSt3R, MASt3R, VGGT, Fast3R, MASt3R-SLAM) benchmarks on a desktop or datacenter NVIDIA GPU with 12–80 GB of video memory drawing 300–700 watts. The Galaxy XR runs a mobile Snapdragon chip with a few watts for compute. The gap is orders of magnitude and no amount of optimisation closes it this year. So SLAM3R is not a runtime engine for your game — it's an *offline authoring tool*. The real architecture is: **scan a place on your PC → reconstruct on a desktop/cloud GPU → bake a finished, decimated, textured world into the app → the headset only renders a pre-built world.** Internalise that and half the hard decisions resolve themselves.

**Reframe 2 — the platform is not the risky part; you're building into a mature stack.** The Galaxy XR is real, buyable hardware ([launched Oct 21 2025, $1,799.99, Snapdragon XR2+ Gen 2, dual micro-OLED at 3,552×3,840 per eye, ~109° field of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samsung_Galaxy_XR)). Unity 6's Android XR support went **general availability on Oct 23 2025** — Google's own launch titles (Google Maps Immersive View, Owlchemy's Job Simulator) shipped on it. Photoreal Gaussian-splat rendering already runs *on-device standalone* on this exact chip class, proven by shipping apps ([Niantic's Into the Scaniverse](https://www.uploadvr.com/niantics-gaussian-splat-scaniverse-is-now-an-app-on-the-quest-store/), [Gracia](https://mixed-news.com/en/gracia-quest-3-hands-on/)). And two apps already do the exact "walk real scanned places in XR" thing you're describing — [BRINK Traveler](https://www.brinkxr.com/) and [Google Maps Immersive View](https://www.uploadvr.com/android-xr-google-maps-best-galaxy-xr/) — natively, on Galaxy XR, today. Your differentiation has to come from *gameplay, procedural planets, and something nobody else has*, not from being first to scanned-world XR.

**Reframe 3 — two of your three names are legally unsafe, and this is not a detail.** "Strange New Worlds" is an [active, currently-airing Paramount series title](https://www.startrek.com/en-un/news/fifth-and-final-season-of-strange-new-worlds) (season 4 premieres July 23 2026). "Wayfinder" is a [live US trademark owned by Airship Syndicate](https://trademarks.justia.com/886/48/wayfinder-88648494.html) (serial 88648494) in exactly the two classes a game needs — downloadable game software and online game services — for a game that's actively selling. Both are hazards as a *public* product name. Keep "Wayfinder" as an internal codename; it's fine on your moodboard, not on the store listing. The good news is in Tier 1.6: the *feeling* of Star Trek exploration is legally free to chase.

## The constraints that shape everything (read before the tiers)

These are the hard numbers your design has to live inside. They're not negotiable if you want the immersive store placement, and several are physical.

| Constraint | The number | What it forces |
|---|---|---|
| Frame budget | [72 fps minimum, 90 fps target](https://developer.android.com/docs/quality-guidelines/android-xr) (13.8 ms / 11.1 ms per frame) | Every world must render inside ~13 ms on a mobile GPU. This is the master constraint. |
| Render resolution | ≥1856×2160 per eye | You're drawing ~8 megapixels/frame stereo before foveation. |
| On-device splat budget | ~200k–400k Gaussians per view *(rule-of-thumb, [SIGGRAPH 2025 course](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3721251.3734056) + [aras-p](https://github.com/aras-p/UnityGaussianSplatting) community figure — must benchmark on the device)* | A splat "planet" can be one room/diorama at a time, not a continuous city. |
| Locomotion rules | [No camera rotation over time, no abrupt camera moves](https://developer.android.com/docs/quality-guidelines/android-xr); playable within a 2.0 m radius | No free smooth-walking a planet. You use teleport / snap-turn / world-grab / on-rails, from a seated or standing spot. This *is* your locomotion design. |
| Input | Hand tracking is the default; must be fully playable with **no controllers** (controllers are a separate $250 add-on) | Design around pinch/grab/gaze first. |
| Asset size | [30 GB cumulative asset packs](https://developer.android.com/develop/xr/package-and-distribute) (vs 4 GB for normal mobile apps) | Big enough to ship several baked worlds via Play Asset Delivery — structure worlds as on-demand downloads. |
| Session length | External tethered battery, ~2–2.5 hrs | Exploration in short loops, not marathon sessions. |
| Your dev machine | The [Android XR "Engine Hub" live-streaming tool is Windows-only](https://android-developers.googleblog.com/2026/06/what-is-new-android-xr.html); you're on macOS | You lose the fastest iterate-against-real-hardware loop unless you keep a Windows box. Plan for it. |
| Licensing landmine | Most reconstruction weights are **non-commercial** | You cannot ship DUSt3R / MASt3R / MASt3R-SLAM / SLAM3R / Fast3R / original-VGGT weights in a paid game. See 1.2 for the narrow set you *can* ship. |

## Tier 1 — Use-today: the shippable spine

Everything here is production-grade right now. A determined solo dev can ship a vertical slice using only Tier 1.

### 1.1 Unity 6 (GA) as the engine — Godot as the free alternative

Unity 6's Android XR build target reached [general availability on Oct 23 2025](https://unity.com/news/unity-powers-launch-titles-for-samsung-galaxy-xr-and-accelerates-the-android-xr-ecosystem-), and Google's own launch titles shipped on it. This is the correction that matters most: an earlier read of the docs suggested "Unity Android XR is still beta" — that's wrong. The *base* path is GA; only the newer Unity 6.5 package (v1.13, the one adding SpaceWarp for UI text and similar polish) is still beta. You can build and publish a real Galaxy XR game on stable Unity today, and one OpenXR build also runs on XREAL's wired AURA glasses and (with a rebuild) Meta Quest — so you're not locked to one headset.

Why it's right for you: a walkable-planet game needs a mature asset pipeline, the community's Gaussian-splat and point-cloud tooling (1.3), foveated rendering, and Application SpaceWarp (frame generation) — Unity has all of it and the launch games prove the path. The plumbing: Unity 6 LTS + `com.unity.xr.androidxr-openxr` (OpenXR provider) + [Android XR Extensions for Unity](https://github.com/android/android-xr-unity-package) + XR Plugin Management + AR Foundation, rendering through the Universal Render Pipeline on Vulkan (required for the advanced graphics features). Input is the [XR Interaction Toolkit + XR Hands package](https://developer.android.com/develop/xr/unity) — hands and eye-gaze are GA. Start from Unity's Mixed Reality template.

The catch most people miss: **you do not need PolySpatial.** PolySpatial is Unity's "app floats in the shared home environment next to other apps" layer (and its visionOS mode needs a paid Unity Pro/Enterprise licence). A fully-immersive explore-a-planet game uses the plain OpenXR + XRI path and skips PolySpatial entirely — simpler and free. The free/open-source alternative is [Godot 4.6.2+ with the Godot OpenXR Vendors plugin 5.1](https://godotengine.org/article/godot-xr-update-may-2026/), which is officially supported with real games already live on Google Play (MoAT, Expedition to Blobotopia). Godot's trade is a much thinner ecosystem for splat/point-cloud/large-world tooling — you'll write more of the world-streaming layer yourself. **Unreal is a trap here**: its Android XR support is [developer-preview only on UE 5.6.1](https://developer.android.com/develop/xr/unreal), Google warns the APIs change between preview releases, and Nanite/Lumen — the whole reason you'd reach for Unreal — aren't realistically usable on a mobile Snapdragon anyway. Prototype the *look* in Unreal if you must; ship on Unity.

### 1.2 The offline reconstruction → clean-mesh pipeline (and the licence minefield)

This is where SLAM3R actually lives in your project. The pipeline is: **capture** (walk a phone/camera through a real place) → **reconstruct** on a desktop/cloud GPU (SLAM3R gives you [20+ fps dense point clouds on a single RTX 4090](https://github.com/pku-vcl-3dv/slam3r), no camera-pose solving) → **mesh** the point cloud → **decimate + UV-unwrap + bake texture** → import the static asset into Unity. The reconstruction net is only step zero; the headset renders a decimated textured mesh, not a 20-million-point cloud.

Why the licence detail is load-bearing: **SLAM3R is built on DUSt3R, so its weights inherit the [CC BY-NC-SA non-commercial licence](https://github.com/naver/mast3r/blob/main/CHECKPOINTS_NOTICE).** So do MASt3R, MASt3R-SLAM, Spann3R, CUT3R, and Fast3R (Meta's non-commercial research licence). You can prototype your pipeline with any of them, but you **cannot legally ship a paid game using their output** until you swap to commercially-usable weights. The narrow commercially-clean set: **[VGGT-1B-Commercial](https://github.com/facebookresearch/vggt)** (the one checkpoint of Meta's flagship model with a commercial licence, military use excluded), **[MoGe / MoGe-2](https://github.com/microsoft/moge)** (MIT-licensed single-image geometry, good for set-dressing), and **[Pi3](https://github.com/yyfz/Pi3)**'s *code* (BSD-3) though its weights are still non-commercial.

The meshing stage is separate and has its own licence catch. Use **[Open3D](http://www.open3d.org/)'s Screened Poisson reconstruction** (`estimate_normals()` then `create_from_point_cloud_poisson()`) — it's MIT-licensed, watertight, and commercially clean. **Do not use MeshLab in the shipped pipeline** — it's GPLv3 (copyleft), which drags obligations into your product; it's fine as a throwaway inspection tool, not part of the build. NVIDIA's [NKSR](https://github.com/nv-tlabs/NKSR) gives higher detail (millions of points → surface in seconds) but ships under NVIDIA's restrictive Source Code License — research/eval only. So: **Open3D Poisson is your safe default.** Then decimate to a mobile budget (~100k–500k triangles per scene chunk, few draw calls) in Blender, UV-unwrap with xatlas, project vertex colours to a texture, export glTF/FBX to Unity.

### 1.3 On-device Gaussian splatting as the runtime look — the "diorama per scene" pattern

For photoreal scanned places, a decimated mesh looks worse than the real thing. **3D Gaussian splatting** (store the scene as millions of tiny coloured blobs, render by sorting and alpha-blending — a job a GPU rasteriser loves) is the representation that both looks better *and* renders on the mobile chip. The proof it works standalone on your exact hardware class: [Into the Scaniverse ships on Quest, rendering user splats on-device](https://voicesofvr.com/1525-niantics-into-the-scaniverse-maps-over-50k-gaussian-splats-from-around-the-world-on-quest-and-webxr/) (50,000+ scenes, no cloud render), and [Gracia](https://mixed-news.com/en/gracia-quest-3-hands-on/) does it commercially.

Your renderer is **[aras-p's UnityGaussianSplatting](https://github.com/aras-p/UnityGaussianSplatting)** — MIT-licensed, actively maintained, and it [gained VR support in Dec 2024, tested standalone on Quest 3](https://radiancefields.com/gaussian-splatting-unity-plugin-gets-vr-support) (needs Vulkan or D3D12, which Android XR has). Capture with **Scaniverse** (free, on-device, exports the compressed `.spz` format) or **Postshot** (Windows desktop trainer, higher quality, fully local). The design pattern Into the Scaniverse proves and you should copy: **one bounded splat scene at a time, swapped as you "travel"** — a diorama per location, not one continuous splatted world. That respects the ~200k–400k-splats-per-view budget (which you *must* benchmark on the real device — it's a rule-of-thumb, not a measured guarantee).

The non-obvious constraint that shapes gameplay: **splats have no collision, don't receive engine lighting or cast shadows, and their lighting is baked-in from capture time.** So every splat world needs an invisible polygon collision mesh under it for the player to walk on, and anything the player touches / that needs dynamic light (creatures, pickups, doors, your AI companion's avatar) has to be a normal PBR mesh actor lit by engine lights. [KIRI Engine's "Mesh-Inclusive 3DGS"](https://www.kiriengine.app/features/mesh-inclusive-3dgs) auto-generates that collision mesh in the splat's coordinate space, solving the hardest part. Plan the game as **splat backdrop + mesh actors** from day one — a fixed sun direction per scene included.

### 1.4 Procedural planets, the classic way — the part you fully own

Generative AI does *not* give you a whole planet (see Tier 2). For the "explore *new* planets" pillar, the proven, ship-today, zero-licence-cost route is classic procedural generation, and it's the part of Wayfinder you own outright rather than rent. Start from **[Sebastian Lague's Procedural Planets](https://github.com/SebLague/Procedural-Planets)** (free, open Unity project): mesh six terrain faces, project them onto a sphere (the cube-sphere / quad-sphere technique real planet games use), stack noise layers for terrain, shade by elevation. It's small enough to understand completely and fork.

Layer on the **[No Man's Sky recipe from the GDC talk](https://www.gdcvault.com/play/1024265/Continuous_World_Generation_in__No_Man_s_Sky_)**: stacked Perlin/simplex noise at multiple frequencies (fractal Brownian motion), domain warping to twist the field into non-repeating shapes, seed-driven biome and colour. This is how you get near-infinite varied planets from math without storing them — the heart of replayability. Implement the noise stack in a compute shader, combine with the cube-sphere meshing, and add chunked level-of-detail + terrain colliders for VR. The catch: it's a technique corpus, not importable code, and VR adds the constraint that terrain chunks must generate inside the frame and thermal budget on a standalone chip. For hand-crafted *hero* regions, [QuadSpinner Gaea 2](https://quadspinner.com/Gaea/Unreal) gives film-grade erosion exported as heightmaps — but it's flat/regional terrain, so use it for landable set-pieces, not the whole globe.

### 1.5 Publishing to Google Play — the concrete path and its gates

Everything you need to publish is live. Pay the [one-time $25 Google Play developer fee](https://support.google.com/googleplay/android-developer/answer/14151465?hl=en) (no XR surcharge), build an **Android App Bundle** from Unity, and publish to the [dedicated "Android XR" release track Google added in Dec 2024](https://android-developers.googleblog.com/2025/10/5-things-you-need-to-know-about.html). A Unity/OpenXR game declares one manifest line — `<uses-feature android:name="android.software.xr.api.openxr" android:required="true" />` — which marks it a fully-immersive "differentiated" XR app, visible only on XR devices and eligible for the [dedicated XR section in the Play Store](https://www.androidcentral.com/apps-software/google-play-store-update-makes-finding-apps-built-for-xr-headsets-way-simpler). You must pass the [Android XR quality guidelines](https://developer.android.com/docs/quality-guidelines/android-xr) (the constraints table above) and fill the Play Console Data Safety form.

Two corrections the fact-check surfaced, both in your favour. First: **Android XR is on the *exempt* list for the Aug 31 2026 "target API 36" deadline** (alongside Wear OS, Android Auto, Android TV) — that deadline that's forcing every phone app to upgrade does not bite XR apps the same way. Second, on money: the [June 30 2026 Play billing restructure](https://android-developers.googleblog.com/2026/06/play-expanded-billing.html) is a 10% service fee on your first $1M/year plus a separate ~5% billing fee when using Play's billing in the US/UK/EEA — roughly a **15% effective cut on your first million**, materially better than Meta's or Apple's 30% storefront cut, and alternative billing / external payment links are now allowed (US, UK, EEA). The one gate to plan around: a **new personal developer account must run a closed test with ≥12 testers for 14 consecutive days** before it can go to production — line up your 12 testers early, or register as an organisation to skip it.

For beta and off-store distribution, Galaxy XR has the [most open sideloading of any major headset](https://www.uploadvr.com/samsung-galaxy-xr-has-easy-sideloading-and-open-bootloader/): download an APK in the on-device browser and install it — no PC, no developer mode, unlockable bootloader, and no separate Samsung store to fight. That's your friction-free playtest channel while the 12-tester clock runs.

### 1.6 The comparables that de-risk the whole plan — and the IP green light

Two shipping apps prove your core loop is buildable on this device: **[BRINK Traveler](https://www.uploadvr.com/brink-traveler-impressions/)** (58 photogrammetry/LiDAR real-world environments you walk inside, native Galaxy XR launch title) and **[Google Maps Immersive View](https://unity.com/resources/google-maps)** (Unity-powered, on Galaxy XR at launch, pinch-drag to pan, two-hand pinch to scale, ask Gemini about a landmark). Study BRINK's locomotion and level-of-detail budget as your baseline; note it uses *pre-captured, hand-cleaned* scans — your SLAM3R idea is the capture front-end, but BRINK-style curation is still needed before a scene ships. The warning from these comparables: [Google Earth VR was abandoned](https://vrpupu.us/2025/10/27/meta-quest-vr-travel-software-guide/) because pure sightseeing without a game loop doesn't sustain. Wayfinder needs to be a *game*, not a museum.

On IP, the research is clear enough to act on. The controlling case is **[Abdin v. CBS](https://www.finnegan.com/en/insights/blogs/incontestable/star-treks-space-traveling-tardigrades-live-long-and-prosper-after-second-circuit-affirms-non-infringement-of-videogame-copyright.html)**, where the Second Circuit affirmed non-infringement over a *space-exploration video game* — the generic idea "explore strange new worlds" is unprotectable, and stock sci-fi is "scenes a faire." So a game inspired by the *feeling* of Star Trek exploration is legally clear, provided you copy none of the specific expression: no Starfleet delta insignia, no Enterprise silhouette, no warp/phaser/tricorder/LCARS, no Klingon/Vulcan, no "United Federation of Planets." Design an original insignia, original faction and tech names, original console UI. Paramount enforces hard even against free fan work ([Axanar sued and settled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Trek_fan_productions); the [Stage 9 fan VR Enterprise-D was killed by cease-and-desist in 2018](https://torrentfreak.com/cbs-shuts-down-stage-9-a-fan-made-recreation-of-the-uss-enterprise-180927/) despite being non-commercial and free) — Stage 9 is the closest analog to what you're building, and it's a graveyard. **Sell the feeling of discovery; never borrow the furniture.** Concrete next step before you announce any public name: a trademark clearance search in classes 9 and 41, plus a Steam/Play/Meta name search, ideally with an IP attorney's opinion. (This is not legal advice.)

## Tier 2 — Use-soon: research-mature, build with care

These are strong, current, and mostly usable — but each carries an "early adopter" tax (per-generation cost, brand-new tooling, or a licence you must double-check).

### 2.1 Generative world models with *exportable* assets — Marble and HY-World 2.0

The single most important split in generative worlds is **exportable-asset vs closed-stream.** The exportable ones you can actually build a game on. **[World Labs' Marble](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model)** (Fei-Fei Li's company, [public since Nov 12 2025](https://techcrunch.com/2025/11/12/fei-fei-lis-world-labs-speeds-up-the-world-model-race-with-marble-its-first-commercial-product/)) turns text / a photo / a video into a persistent navigable 3D world and *exports triangle meshes and Gaussian splats* — a studio already imported Marble splats into Unreal for a virtual-production stage. Use it to turn a concept image of a place into an explorable region or a hero landmark. Pricing: Free (4 worlds/mo), Standard $20/mo, **Pro $35/mo is the tier with commercial rights + high-res mesh export**, Max $95/mo. The catch: worlds are room/region scale (not planets), splats are heavy for standalone XR, and the credit model means iterating costs real money.

The open-source counterpart is **[Tencent HY-World 2.0](https://github.com/Tencent-Hunyuan/HY-World-2.0)** ([open-sourced Apr 15 2026, Apache 2.0](https://kr-asia.com/tencents-hy-world-2-0-moves-ai-beyond-video-into-editable-3d-worlds)), which from one prompt or photo outputs **mesh + 3D Gaussian splats + point cloud + depth + camera params** — the widest export menu of any model, all dropping into Unity/Unreal, and self-hostable so there's no per-world cost or lock-in. Its predecessor [HunyuanWorld 1.0](https://github.com/Tencent-Hunyuan/HunyuanWorld-1.0) (Jul 2025) is also mesh-exporting. Both generate offline on a workstation GPU (same as SLAM3R), and auto-generated mesh topology needs cleanup — but for one-off explorable scenes and landmark set-pieces you fully own, this is the pick. Choose **mesh output for on-device performance, splats only for fidelity backdrops.**

### 2.2 Single-object generative 3D — the prop/creature/ship workhorse

For everything scattered *across* your planets — rocks, plants, alien artifacts, your original (non-Enterprise) ships, creatures — use image/text-to-3D. **[Microsoft TRELLIS 2](https://comfyui-wiki.com/en/news/2025-12-18-microsoft-trellis2-3d-generation)** (4-billion-parameter, [released Dec 2025, MIT-licensed](https://trellis2.app/blog/comfyui-3d-model-generator-microsoft)) turns one photo into a textured, game-ready GLB in seconds inside ComfyUI — free and unrestricted, ideal for a solo build. The hosted commercial options each have a niche: **Tripo** is fastest and includes rigging/animation (good for skeletal NPCs/creatures), **Rodin** and **Meshy** give cleaner production topology, all export GLB for Unity. Pair any of them with a decimation/LOD step for the mobile budget. The limit is inherent: one object per generation, not a scene — these populate worlds, they don't build them.

### 2.3 The reconstruction frontier — VGGT, Fast3R, MASt3R-SLAM

Beyond SLAM3R, the family matured hard. **[VGGT (Visual Geometry Grounded Transformer)](https://github.com/facebookresearch/vggt)** — Meta+Oxford, CVPR 2025 Best Paper — is the flagship: from N photos it predicts camera poses, depth, dense point maps and 3D tracks in one pass (1.2B params, a scene in under a second, ~60 frames in 24 GB VRAM). Crucially it's the **one top-tier model with a commercial checkpoint (VGGT-1B-Commercial)**, so it's the shippable choice for turning a photo set of a real place into geometry. **[Fast3R](https://fast3r-3d.github.io/)** processes up to 1,500 images in a single forward pass (where DUSt3R runs out of memory past ~48) — the pick when you photograph a whole location as stills; but its weights are non-commercial. **[MASt3R-SLAM](https://edexheim.github.io/mast3r-slam/)** gives you accurate camera trajectory *plus* dense geometry from a moving camera (~15 fps on a 4090) — useful if you want to drive an in-game path through the scanned space — also non-commercial. Build-with-care because: the VRAM ceilings mean long captures must be tiled, point maps are noisy at depth edges and need cleanup, and the licence split (commercial only for VGGT-1B-Commercial) is a real gate.

### 2.4 The mobile-GPU performance levers — SpaceWarp, foveation, and Unity 6.5's beta perf features

The tools that make dense worlds hold framerate on the mobile chip. **Application SpaceWarp** (on-device frame generation — render at half rate, synthesise the in-between frames) is supported in Unity via the Android XR Extensions and is the biggest single lever for heavy scenes; the newer [Unity 6.5 beta package extended it to UI](https://android-developers.googleblog.com/2026/05/android-xr-updates-unity-unreal-godot.html). **Dynamic foveated rendering** (the eye-tracking cameras let you render full detail only where the eye looks) is the second lever, and it's exactly how [VRSplat](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.10144) claws back frame budget. Build-with-care because SpaceWarp isn't free quality — it needs correct motion vectors and depth, and dense reconstructed geometry with bad motion vectors produces visible artifacts. Budget engineering time to tune it, and validate on the real device (the emulator won't tell you on-device framerate).

## Tier 3 — Bet-on: a 12-month moat if you build it

This is the differentiated part. Almost nobody is doing these in a shipping game yet. Do them and Wayfinder stands apart from BRINK Traveler and every other scanned-world app.

### 3.1 A Gemini-powered in-world companion — your "ship's computer"

This is the single biggest thing you have that no prior VR platform did. **Gemini, Google's multimodal AI, is integrated at the OS level of Android XR** and reachable by developers via the [Gemini Live API](https://developer.android.com/develop/xr/gemini) — real-time voice + vision. No headset before this had a first-party multimodal AI wired into the system. For Wayfinder that means a **companion that literally sees what the player sees and reacts** — a Star-Trek-style science officer or ship's computer that narrates the planet you're looking at, answers "what is that structure?", logs discoveries, reacts to what you find. Google Maps Immersive View already ships the primitive version ("ask Gemini about the Colosseum you're looking at"); nobody's built it as a *game companion* yet.

The honest caveat that keeps this in Bet-on rather than Use-today: the clean, documented path for calling Gemini Live *from inside a Unity/Unreal immersive game* is not spelled out in the mid-2026 docs — the previewed integrations are with ARCore Geospatial and the glasses form factor. You'll likely pioneer it as a streaming/REST client from within your engine app, and you'll have to design around latency, per-call cost, and the privacy reality that a camera feed goes to the cloud (disclose it in the Data Safety form). But this is the item that turns "another scanned-world walker" into "the exploration game with a mind in it." Bet on it.

### 3.2 Breaking the diorama ceiling — mesh-bound and LOD splats

The Tier 1 splat pattern caps you at one room-sized diorama per scene. The research that lifts that cap is arriving. **[NanoGS](https://www.cgchannel.com/2026/03/free-plugin-nanogs-puts-nanite-style-gaussian-splatting-in-unreal-engine/)** (free, ~Mar 2026) brings Nanite-style level-of-detail clustering to splats — draw only visible splats at screen-appropriate detail, the key to a large walkable area. **[Voyager](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.02774)** (Jun 2025) does temporal LOD + alpha-filtering for city-scale splats on mobile — up to 6.6× speedup, 85% energy savings. **Mesh-bound splat methods** (GS-Verse, MeshSplats, KIRI's mesh-inclusive) bind Gaussians to a mesh so physics, deformation, collision, and coherent lighting become possible — meaning a splat creature or destructible splat prop, not just static backdrop. **[SqueezeMe](https://arxiv.org/html/2412.15171v1)** (Meta, Dec 2024) already runs three ~60k-splat avatars simultaneously on-device on Quest 3. Bet-on because these are mostly papers and brand-new plugins with no proven-on-Galaxy-XR numbers — but they map the path from "photo of a room" to "walkable district," and building tooling around them now is a genuine lead.

### 3.3 Cloud-streamed splats — the escape hatch from the on-device budget

If you want photoreal worlds *beyond* the ~400k-splat on-device ceiling, the architecture that delivers it is cloud rendering. **[Meta Hyperscape / Project Avalanche](https://techcrunch.com/2025/09/17/meta-launches-hyperscape-technology-to-turn-real-world-spaces-into-vr/)** (Sept 2025) renders whole photoreal splat rooms in Meta's cloud and streams them to the headset as a thin client — quality no standalone GPU can match locally, because it isn't rendered locally. For a planet-exploration game this could deliver far larger, richer worlds. The bet: it's Meta's closed system (not an open SDK for third-party worlds), so *for you* this is a "build your own splat-streaming backend" bet — real server cost, network latency, an always-online requirement — or a "wait for someone to offer it as a service" track. It defines where the ceiling moves; a solo dev can't trivially replicate it, but it's worth knowing the on-device budget isn't the final word if the game takes off.

### 3.4 Next-gen reconstruction — Pi3 and MapAnything

If Wayfinder is a longer build, bet on the newest reconstruction models. **[Pi3 (π³)](https://github.com/yyfz/Pi3)** (ICLR 2026) drops the fixed-reference-frame dependence of DUSt3R/VGGT (it's permutation-equivariant), giving more robust, smoother pose + depth + point maps — and it's the **first top model to split its licensing so the *code* is BSD-3 (commercial-friendly)** even though the weights are still non-commercial, which means you can build your pipeline on it legally and train or source commercial weights separately. **[MapAnything](https://map-anything.github.io/)** (Meta, Sept 2025) is a universal metric model doing 12+ geometry tasks with swappable backends — the "one model, many input types" convenience layer. Bet-on because both are new with rougher tooling than VGGT, but they're where reconstruction quality is heading.

## Tier 4 — Speculative: probably 18+ months out, but worth tracking

### 4.1 Real-time interactive world models (Genie 3 / Project Genie)

The flashiest "walk into any world you describe" demo. **[Google DeepMind's Genie 3](https://deepmind.google/blog/genie-3-a-new-frontier-for-world-models/)** generates a navigable photorealistic world frame-by-frame as you move; the consumer [Project Genie launched Jan 29 2026](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/models-and-research/google-deepmind/project-genie/) to US Google AI Ultra subscribers. Why not yet: it streams 720p at 24 fps, the world stays consistent for only ~1 minute (then drifts and forgets), there's **no mesh/splat/asset export**, and it runs cloud-only. It's a dead end as a *build material* for a shipped game today — but it's exactly where the field is heading, and the day it becomes exportable and persistent, the "infinite explorable planets" problem changes shape. Track it.

### 4.2 On-device neural reconstruction

The day the headset can scan-and-reconstruct a world live, the offline-bake architecture becomes optional. **[QuantVGGT](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.21302)** (4-bit quantised VGGT, ~3.7× memory reduction, ~2.5× speedup, no quality loss) is the first serious step — but it still targets *desktop* GPUs and is research-only. The Snapdragon XR2+ does have a hardware CV/reconstruction block, but it's for lightweight perception, not billion-parameter geometry transformers. Worth tracking because it's the single change that would let a player scan their own room into the game in real time — a killer feature the moment it's feasible.

### 4.3 Interactive-video world models (Odyssey, Decart Oasis)

[Odyssey-2 and Decart Oasis](https://decart.ai/) stream controllable worlds one frame at a time. Same closed-stream limitation as Genie 3 — no exportable geometry, cloud-only, per-second API pricing — and Oasis is scoped to driving/AV simulation, not exploration. Relevant only if Wayfinder ever became a cloud-rendered game, which contradicts the standalone-headset premise. Track lightly.

### 4.4 Real-astronomical-data planets

Not frontier *tech* so much as a frontier *content* bet: [Elite Dangerous seeds ~160,000 systems from real star catalogues](https://forums.frontier.co.uk/threads/elite-dangerous-cobra-engine-updatable.595765/) via its Stellar Forge generator. If Wayfinder wants scientifically-grounded planets, real exoplanet and terrain datasets are a route that gives authenticity procedural noise can't — at the cost of building a data pipeline. Worth knowing because "these are *real* worlds" is a differentiator no purely-procedural competitor can claim, and the datasets are public.

## The single thing I'd build first

If I had one engineer and one month, and had to pick the single highest-leverage move:

**Ship a vertical slice of one bounded, hand-picked scanned real place — rendered as an on-device Gaussian splat with a collision mesh under it, in Unity 6 (GA), with a Gemini Live companion that reacts to what you look at — sideloaded onto the Galaxy XR.**

That one slice proves the entire spine end to end using only shippable-today pieces: the engine path, on-device photoreal rendering, the splat+mesh hybrid, the locomotion rules, and — the part nobody else has — an AI that inhabits the world with you. It replaces:

- the assumption that you need SLAM3R at runtime (you don't — it's offline authoring, and the slice can even start from a Scaniverse capture with no reconstruction at all),
- the assumption that you need Unreal for fidelity (you don't — splats out-look decimated Nanite meshes on this chip and run standalone),
- and the assumption that a scanned-world walker is enough (it isn't — Google Earth VR died proving that; the Gemini companion is the game).

Procedural planets (1.4), your own reconstruction pipeline (1.2), and generative worlds (2.1) are all refinements you layer *after* the loop is proven. But the splat-diorama-plus-AI-companion slice is the piece that tells you in four weeks whether Wayfinder is a game or a tech demo — and it's the piece that, if it lands, no other Galaxy XR exploration app currently has.

One housekeeping item that isn't optional: **pick a different public name before you build any brand around it.** "Wayfinder" and "Strange New Worlds" are both taken in ways that will cost you. The exploration *feeling* is yours to chase; those two words aren't.
